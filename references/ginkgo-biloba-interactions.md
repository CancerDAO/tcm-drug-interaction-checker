# Ginkgo biloba Drug Interactions

## Overview

Ginkgo biloba is one of the oldest living tree species, with extracts from its leaves and seeds used for cognitive enhancement, circulatory disorders, and antioxidant effects. However, it has significant interactions with anticoagulant and antiplatelet therapies.

## Key Interaction Mechanism

### Antiplatelet/Anticoagulant Effects

Ginkgo biloba contains **ginkgolides** (particularly ginkgolide B) which:
- Inhibit platelet-activating factor (PAF) receptor
- Reduce platelet aggregation
- Prolong bleeding time
- May increase risk of hemorrhage

## Drug Interactions

### Anticoagulants/Antiplatelets - MAJOR CONCERN

| Drug Class | Specific Drug | Interaction | Severity |
|------------|---------------|-------------|----------|
| **Warfarin** | Warfarin | Increased INR, bleeding risk | Major |
| **Heparin** | All heparins | Additive anticoagulant effect | Major |
| **DOACs** | Apixaban, rivaroxaban, dabigatran | Variable, bleeding risk | Major |
| **Aspirin** | All aspirin doses | Additive antiplatelet effect | Moderate-Major |
| **Clopidogrel** | Clopidogrel | Additive antiplatelet effect | Moderate-Major |
| **NSAIDs** | Ibuprofen, naproxen, etc. | GI bleeding risk added | Moderate |

### Mechanism-Based Interactions
| Enzyme/Transporter | Effect | Clinical Concern |
|--------------------|--------|------------------|
| **CYP450** | Mild induction | Generally low concern |
| **P-gp** | Substrate, mild inhibitor | Variable effects |

## Cancer-Specific Considerations

### Surgery
**CRITICAL**: Discontinue ginkgo biloba at least **2 weeks before any surgical procedure** due to bleeding risk.

### Chemotherapy
- Some chemotherapy agents cause myelosuppression
- Bleeding risk compounds with thrombocytopenia
- Theoretical concern with drugs affecting coagulation

### Antiangiogenic Therapies
Some targeted therapies have antiangiogenic properties:
- Combined with ginkgo may theoretically increase bleeding risk
- Not well studied, but exercise caution

## Severity Classification for Bleeding Risk

| Risk Level | Patient Characteristics | Ginkgo Use |
|-----------|------------------------|------------|
| **High Risk** | On anticoagulants, antiplatelets, or with bleeding disorders | **AVOID** |
| **Moderate Risk** | Older adults, history of GI bleeding, on NSAIDs | Use with extreme caution |
| **Low Risk** | No bleeding risk factors | Use with awareness of bleeding risk |

## Clinical Recommendations

### Before Surgery
1. **Discontinue ginkgo 14 days** before elective surgery
2. Inform surgeon and anesthesiologist of ginkgo use
3. Consider bleeding time/prothrombin time if surgery cannot be delayed

### For Patients on Anticoagulants
1. **Avoid ginkgo** if taking warfarin, DOACs, or heparins
2. If patient insists on using, must have close INR monitoring
3. Document discussion of bleeding risks

### For Cancer Patients
1. Assess bleeding risk from disease (e.g., bone marrow involvement, liver mets)
2. Consider thrombocytopenia from chemotherapy
3. Generally avoid ginkgo during active cancer treatment

## Adverse Effects

### Bleeding-Related
- Intracranial hemorrhage (rare but documented)
- GI bleeding
- Increased bleeding at wound sites
- Hyphema (bleeding in eye)
- Hemarthrosis (bleeding into joints)

### Other Adverse Effects
- GI upset
- Headache
- Allergic skin reactions
- Seizures (in susceptible individuals)
