# Drug Interaction Severity Classification Guide

## Overview

Drug interaction severity classification systems help healthcare providers and patients understand the clinical significance of potential interactions and guide appropriate action. This guide summarizes the standard classification systems and their clinical application.

## Major Classification Systems

### 1. Lexicomp/Lexicomp Severity Scale

The most widely used clinical classification system:

| Severity Level | Definition | Clinical Action |
|---------------|------------|------------------|
| **Contraindicated** | Combination should be avoided | Do not use together |
| **Major** | May cause potential harm; may require intervention | Avoid combination or use with extreme caution |
| **Moderate** | May result in outcome deterioration; may require monitoring | Use with caution; monitor closely |
| **Minor** | Limited clinical effect; may enhance beneficial effect | Generally safe; be aware |

### 2. FDA Classification for Drug Interactions

**Category A** - No known interactions (no FDA category; implied when not labeled)
**Category B** - Usually not clinically significant
**Category C** - Monitor therapy
**Category D** - May require monitoring; risk outweighs benefit in some cases
**Category X** - Contraindicated; avoid combination

### 3. Clinical Pharmacology Interaction Classification

**Type A** - Predictable, pharmacokinetic interactions
**Type B** - Unpredictable, often dose-dependent
**Type C** - Long-term effects on drug concentrations
**Type D** - Delayed toxicity (discovered years after marketing)

## Mechanism-Based Severity Assessment

### Pharmacokinetic Interactions

| Mechanism | Severity Factors | Example |
|-----------|------------------|---------|
| **CYP450 Inhibition** | Potency of inhibitor, therapeutic index of victim drug | Major when inhibitor is potent and drug has narrow TI |
| **CYP450 Induction** | Time to steady-state, drug half-life | Moderate-Major; delayed onset |
| **P-gp Inhibition** | Location (gut vs systemic), drug TI | Moderate-Major |
| **Protein Binding Displacement** | Usually minor unless high protein binding | Minor-Moderate |

### Pharmacodynamic Interactions

| Type | Severity | Example |
|------|----------|---------|
| **Additive effects** | Depends on therapeutic window | Moderate-High |
| **Synergistic effects** | Potentially major | Major if toxic |
| **Antagonistic effects** | Depends on therapeutic goal | Moderate-Major |

## Severity Assessment for Specific Drug Classes

### High-Risk Drug Classes (Interactions more likely to be Major/Contraindicated)

| Drug Class | Reason | Examples |
|-----------|--------|----------|
| **Warfarin** | Narrow therapeutic index | Bleeding risk |
| **Digoxin** | Narrow therapeutic index | Toxicity |
| **Immunosuppressants** | Transplant rejection risk | Cyclosporine, tacrolimus |
| **MAOI antidepressants** | Tyramine crisis, serotonin syndrome | Phenelzine, tranylcypromine |
| **Chemotherapy** | Life-threatening toxicity or loss of efficacy | Many agents |

### Moderate-Risk Drug Classes

| Drug Class | Concern Level | Management |
|-----------|---------------|------------|
| **Statins** | Muscle toxicity | Monitor CK, hold if surgery |
| **SSRIs** | Bleeding, serotonin syndrome | Monitor, avoid combinations |
| **Beta-blockers** | Bradycardia, hypotension | Monitor heart rate/BP |
| **ACE inhibitors** | Hyperkalemia, hypotension | Monitor potassium/BP |

## Clinical Decision Framework

### Step 1: Identify Mechanism
- Is it pharmacokinetic (CYP, P-gp, absorption)?
- Is it pharmacodynamic (additive, synergistic, antagonistic)?
- Is it combined (both mechanisms)?

### Step 2: Assess Clinical Significance
Consider:
- Therapeutic index of affected drug
- Disease being treated (serious vs minor)
- Available alternatives
- Patient-specific factors (age, renal/hepatic function)

### Step 3: Determine Action
| If Severity Is... | Action |
|-------------------|--------|
| **Contraindicated** | Do not use together |
| **Major** | Avoid if possible; if necessary, monitor very closely |
| **Moderate** | Use with caution; implement monitoring plan |
| **Minor** | Generally safe; note for awareness |

### Step 4: Document and Monitor
- Document the interaction and decision in patient record
- Educate patient on signs to watch for
- Establish monitoring plan with appropriate timelines

## Special Populations

### Cancer Patients - Additional Considerations
- **Narrow therapeutic indices** of many chemotherapy agents
- **Myelosuppression** makes bleeding/infection risks higher
- **Altered pharmacokinetics** due to tumor effects on organs
- **Supportive care drugs** (antiemetics, growth factors) also have interactions

### Older Adults
- Polypharmacy increases interaction probability
- Reduced hepatic/renal function affects drug metabolism
- Increased sensitivity to adverse effects
